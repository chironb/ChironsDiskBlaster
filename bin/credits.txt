/* Images - Overview */
/*****************************************************************************/

All the image icons of systems and drives were modified to make them look less
yellowed due to photodegradation, to make logo colors pop once they are reduced
to icon size, to remove scratches and scuffs, and finally isolated from any 
background.

/* Images - Modified Images from Wikimedia */
/*****************************************************************************/

The following files were heavily modified. In some cases I have rebuilt major
parts of the image that were covered or flipped images to appear at an angle
matching the other photos which involved rebuilding the parts that would
look odd being flipped. I will try to outline the major reworks below. For 
everything else the images were simply reduced in size, enhanced for contrast
and color at an icon size, and otherwise tweaked or had LEDs artifically 
illuminated.

- APPLE_BELLHOWELL.png was replicated from the Wikipedia image of a common
  Apple II Plus. The logo was made bigger and brighter to make it pop once
  it was reduced to icon size. 

- APPLE_III.png was heavily edited to make it look less yellow due to 
  photodegradation while making the colorful labels and logo pop. 
  Although all images were treated to removing yellowing, this particular
  image needed a lot of it, so I am mentioning it here. 

- CBM_C64GS.png was very heavily changed. The original image was partially 
  covered by a joystick and was facing turned in the other direction compared
  to the other images. I rebuilt almost the entire image to replace the 
  missing parts as well as flipping it around and then unflipped the elements
  that would not look correct if they were not flipped back. For example the 
  labels would need to be restored so the text is readable. In any case if 
  one compares the original image of the one in the program it might appear
  to be a completely different image taken from a different angle. This is why.

- ATARIST_ST354.png and ATARIST_ST314.png are from the referenced Wikimedia 
  image. However they were heavily modified since they are partially covered
  up by the computer in front of them in the original picture. Additionally 
  only one kind of drive appears in the photo. The other drive image was 
  fabricated by modifying the LED and eject button to resemble the other 
  model. Note: The external drive was completely fabracted with details 
  mentioned in the next section. 

- DRAGON_32.png, DRAGON_64.png, and DRAGON_TANO.png are all the same photo 
  with recreations of the different labels applied. 

- OLIVETTI_M20_525_DRIVE.png was completely fabricated. I could not find a 
  single example of an Olivetti external drive anywhere. It is just the 
  drive face from the Olivetti image grafted onto the image of an clone
  Apple Disk ][ enclosure by Shamrock, a Canadian Apple-II clone company.

/* Images - Additional Creations from My Photos */
/*****************************************************************************/

The following files were created by from photos of physical mockups or by
taking one or more of my photos and reworking it to appear to be a different
system or drive. I am making notes of this below so that someone does not get
the impression there is some rare vintage computer hardware that has been
previously undocumented. 

- AMIGA_1500.png was created from a photo of my Amiga 2000.

- BBC_MICRO.png is of an unmodified North-American edition. This is being 
  mentioned because it is extremely rare and therefore might have some 
  previously undocumented design changes. However, at the size the icons are 
  displayed at, this should be of little consequence. But just in case!

- MAC_128K was made by modifying a photo of my Macintosh Plus. 

- CBM_128D.png was created by starting with photos of the Amiga 1000 and also 
  a photo of a Commodore 1571 disk drive. I do not have a Commodore 128D 
  and could not find an image online that would look good so this was 
  fabricated. Since this is a rare machine I want to make sure nobody is out 
  there looking for some rare prototype 128D that does not exist because of
  what is effectively a totally fabricated image. 

- PC_80286.png is actually a picture of a Commodore PC10-II which did not 
  have HD 5.25-inch drives but later models did and at this same icon size
  it looks like AT era machines that would have had a HD 5.25-inch drive.

- PC_80486.png is an actual photo of the first PC I ever built for myself 
  back in high-school around 1995 or so. That design of case was very common
  for that era and many PC compatible machines continued to use cases that 
  were actually like that or very similar. Since PC compatibles are so 
  varied it is hard to find something to represent each era. 
  This machine will works by-the-way!

- AMIGA_DRIVE_A4K.png was created from a photo of my Amiga 4000 internal 
  high-density drive that was grafted onto a photo of an external enclosure. 
  It was intended to replicate the look of an actual real drive that exists
  without perfectly replicating it. Therefore do not look for this drive in 
  real life as it does not exist.

- ACORN_35_DRIVE_LIGHT.png, ACORN_35_DRIVE_LIGHT_2.png and 
  ACORN_35_DRIVE_DARK.png were all just photos of my external drives in
  enclosures I designed and 3D printed for drives I use with Greaseweazle. 
  I grafted on the Acorn logo to make it match the system image but these
  drives never existed. In fact I do not know of any example of an Acorn
  Archimedes external floppy drive. Therefore consider these illustrative.

- ATARIST_INT.png was fabricted just like the Amiga HD external drive. In 
  fact that image was the starting point. The face of the drive was then 
  changed to match the look of the internal HD 3.5-inch drives in Atari ST
  machines. 

- BBC_DRIVE_525_SS.png was created by cobbling together a mock-up of this 
  drive and then the image was modified to match the actual BBC Micro 
  drive as it has a different look than a typical Shugart drive and needs
  the BBC Micro logo. 

- BBC_DRIVE_525_DS.png is a picture of my actual BBC Micro drive from my
  North American machine. That is an actual 5.25-inch 80-track drive and 
  came with the Acorn logo on the front. I have not seen any pictures of 
  that drive online so I have no idea if it is rare like the computer
  or common. 

- CBM_DRIVE_2040.png, CBM_DRIVE_3040.png, and CBM_DRIVE_4040.png are actually
  not quite what they appear. I have two 4040 drives. One is VERY early with
  low serial number around 160 and actually runs the same CBM DOS that the 
  2040 and 3040 run. It also does not have a power LED like most 4040 drives
  do, nor does it have the "drive 0" and "drive 1" labels that most other 
  4040 drives have. So I took a photo of my early 4040 and made that the 
  2040 and 3040 drives. I then took at picture of my later 4040 and that is
  the 4040 used for the icon picture in this program. 

- CBM_DRIVE_4031.png is actually my CBM_DRIVE_2031.png but with a slight 
  edit to make the label look like a 4031. 

- CBM_DRIVE_1551.png and CBM_DRIVE_1570.png are total fabrications made from 
  photos of my 1541 and SFD-1001. I do not own either drive so this was 
  needed and since they look similar is design it was better than using this 
  as an excuse to aquire yet another Commodore floppy disk drive. 

- CBM_DRIVE_1581.png is a photo of my drive. However, I would like to note that
  this is drive is a recreation. I bought a real Commodore 1581 empty unused case
  from when Commodore was scrapping 1581 drive meches for use in Amiga sales
  and there was a lot for sales as surplus for many years. I also aquired a 
  broken 1571 and used that for most of the need chips as I bought a replica 
  PCB. I put an Amiga drive inside just as an original would and got a little
  original PCB with the front LEDs from Tom Luff and was able to finally  
  assemble a 1581 where the only part that was a replica was the PCB. Well...
  ...the power connector was a MIDI connector I melted into a 4-pin DIN that
  fits the Commodore 1581 power supply connector. :-)

- CBM_DRIVE_8250.png is actually a photo of my 8050 used for CBM_DRIVE_8050.png   
  but modified with different LEDs illuminated so that it looks a little 
  different since both drives look very similar. 

- CBM_DRIVE_SFD1001.png is a picture of my drive that got from Sid Bolton and 
  came with device number switches modded on the side. I modified them for them
  picture but I have kept that drive as it was when I got it from Sid. It has
  been repaired but other than that it is still the way it was when I got it. 

- PC_DRIVE_525_DD.png is a picture of a 5.25-inch DD drive I have. I think that
  black drives are a reflection of what was common during this era. 
  PC compatibles are so common and varied I simply tried to find examples that
  would reflect what was most common. 
       I also added a DD logo for this both to make it clear and because it 
  would be impossible to know for a PC drive otherwise. This logo is a complete 
  invention of myself and does not actually exist elsewhere. I tried to match the
  look and feel of the HD logo to make something that matches yet makes it clear
  what the user is looking at. PC drives were so varried that any picture would 
  not make it clear what kind of drive it was in the same way a picture of a 
  Commodore 1541 would be clear. Note: I created SD, DD, QD all in the HD style. 

- PC_DRIVE_525_HD.png is a picture of a 5.25-inch DD drive I have. It is a 
  light beige color which most of the HD drives from that era were so I chose
  this drive to represent that even though it is really just a DD drive. 
       I also added the HD logo for this both to make it clear and because it 
  would be impossible to know for a PC drive otherwise. This logo was only ever
  used on 3.5-inch HD disks to make it clear what they were. Using it like 
  this never happened in real life. I have simplyed used this to make things
  as clear as possible for the user. PC drives were so varried that any picture
  would not make it clear what kind of drive it was in the same way a picture 
  of a Commodore 1541 would be clear. 

- PC_DRIVE_35_DD.png is a picture of a 3.5-inch HD drive I have. I chose this 
  because the look of an external drive that like to me, looks, very old and 
  matches the DD era of these drives. 
       I also added a DD logo for this both to make it clear and because it 
  would be impossible to know for a PC drive otherwise. This logo is a complete 
  invention of myself and does not actually exist elsewhere. I tried to match the
  look and feel of the HD logo to make something that matches yet makes it clear
  what the user is looking at. PC drives were so varried that any picture would 
  not make it clear what kind of drive it was in the same way a picture of a 
  Commodore 1541 would be clear. 

- PC_DRIVE_35_HD.png is a picture of a 3.5-inch HD drive I have. I chose this 
  drive because it is the most common looking HD drive that would have never
  actually been an external drive like this. Therefore this look alludes to 
  the drives that were almost exclusively installed in PC cases at this point 
  in history. I think it works. 
       I also added the HD logo for this both to make it clear and because it 
  would be impossible to know for a PC drive otherwise. They were so varried
  that any picture would not make it clear what kind of drive it was in the 
  same way a picture of a Commodore 1541 would be clear.
